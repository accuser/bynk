# Editorial brief

## Working proposition

Most service architectures depend on conventions the compiler cannot see:
diagrams, folder structures, branded strings, dependency-injection rules,
ownership assumptions, authorisation middleware, and testing discipline. Bynk
asks which of those conventions should instead be language constructs, and what
becomes possible when the compiler understands them.

## Reader

The primary reader is an experienced TypeScript or backend developer who has
felt the gap between a service's intended architecture and the code that
implements it. The reader need not have decided to adopt Bynk.

Secondary readers include software architects and programming-language
designers interested in language-level approaches to service design.

## Promise

The book will help the reader recognise where architectural information is lost
in ordinary service code, understand Bynk's response, and assess the benefits
and costs of making those decisions compiler-visible.

## This book is

- a narrative argument about difficult service-design problems;
- an examination of Bynk as one coherent response;
- grounded in realistic systems, successful programs, and compiler refusals;
- candid about constraints, omissions, and alternatives.

## This book is not

- the online Bynk Book in print;
- an exhaustive language reference;
- a linear installation tutorial;
- a catalogue of features;
- a claim that every service should be written in Bynk.

## Working title

**The architecture is the program**

*Why services become hard to reason about - and Bynk's answer*

Both are provisional.

## Current scale

Aim initially at 45,000-60,000 words and approximately 180-240 finished pages.
Let the completed argument determine the final length.
